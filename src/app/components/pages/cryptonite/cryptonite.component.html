Cryptonite

Role: UI/UX Design, Creative Code (Three.js, WebGL), Web Development

Tools: HTML/CSS, JavaScript, Sketch

A human-centered design fellowship with IDEO.

Background

In Winter 2018, I joined IDEO as a CoLab fellow focused on using human-centered design to explore how emerging technologies will affect major societal systems. In this fellowship, I was placed in a multidisciplinary team and collaborated on designing provocations and prototypes for two consecutive design sprints.

Team

With backgrounds spanning energy, design thinking, mechatronics research, product management, and venture design, my amazing teammates and I were assigned to work with the blockchain and gamifying unique digital objects. On the team, I was a strategist, designer, and developer. I designed and programmed the web application of the prototype, conducted user interviews, and helped lead venture strategy.

Prompt

Our team’s prompt was to design and implement a BattleBots-style game that features collectible items that can be tracked and traded on the Ethereum blockchain. In unpacking our design brief, we were able to, at a high-level, identify the affordances that the blockchain may bring to a BattleBots-style game. Games are fantastic tools for learning, understanding, and creating joyful experiences. Collectibility can add excitement to games (ie Pogs, Magic: the Gathering, or Overwatch), and the fact that some items are rarer than others is what makes you want to collect them. Blockchains let us track the issuance and transfer of unique digital collectibles on a global scale. The interplay of these concepts formed the basis of our team's ideas.

Ideation

In order to understand the context for which we were designing, we mapped out the user’s journey for playing a game that involves the creation, collection, and interaction of unique digital objects (whatever the content of the objects may be). We outlined the people, situations, environments, and behaviors that exist today in order to identify the key moments that we wanted to prototype for user feedback.

By mapping out the user’s activities, we were able to highlight the opportunity of prototyping the battle’s “win” moment.

We brainstormed a ton of ideas for the bot’s identity, attributes, and mechanics before narrowing in.

How might we gamify the creation, collection, and interaction between unique digital objects?

Exploratory Research

At this point, we knew our goal, but had many unknowns that loomed over our heads. We decided to cluster our questions in order to figure an area to dive deeper on. In this process, we realized that our primary questions were, “How does the blockchain enable unique, engaging game mechanics? What value, if any, does the blockchain provide in this context? Is it even necessary?” So, we decided to create a simple paper prototype in order to make our questions real enough to effectively communicate to others. We defined a temporary visual identity for the bot in order to allow people to focus on the game mechanics and collectibles. For this early prototype, we modeled the bot after the popular Snapchat sticker pack “Seedlings” because they are animalesque, but abstract enough to easily breed with each other.

We also prototyped a basic instruction manual that illustrates the bot’s characteristics, the game mechanics, and the collectibles’ (charms, in this case) breeding abilities.

Feedback

We solicited feedback from 25+ people and started by asking open-ended questions such as “Think out loud and tell us what you see or don’t see,” “How would you buy a bot,” and “How could we improve the battle experience?” We talked to game designers, entrepreneurs, engineers, blockchain experts, gamers, collectible-card enthusiasts, and people who work in entirely unrelated industries. We measured both explicit feedback in the form of quotes and behavioral feedback in the form of observation.

Our next step was to identify themes by clustering and prioritizing the feedback we received. In this process, we realized that we were focusing on the wrong questions. Instead of trying to understand how the blockchain provides value to the game itself (which was not compelling to our testers and strayed from the prompt), we needed to focus on the true value of creating a BattleBots-style game on the blockchain: the collectibles. Our next step was to revisit the prompt, talk to people who are involved with collectibles to better understand their motivations, and to focus on creating a compelling metaphor for the bot.

Iteration

Research

Our early, rough prototypes pushed our understanding of cryptocollectibles and allowed us to understand the true value proposition behind the prompt.

Using the blockchain as the source of truth for ownership enables us to evoke the emotional, speculative, and utility value of digital collectibles.

We interviewed people interested in collectibles (i.e. Cryptokitties, Magic: the Gathering, Baseball cards, Pogs, etc) to discover what motivates them to collect. With this knowledge, we created a framework for developing the components of the game with a sensitivity to these motivations.

Mapping the values of digital collectibles to the components of our game.

A revised user journey based off newfound insights from our interviews.

Metaphor

Once we understood the value of cryptocollectibles in relation to our game components, we were able to create a narrative.

We decided to use planets as our metaphor because the familiar structure and visual nature of space makes it compelling and easy to understand. Users collect planets to fill their galaxy, battle other planets to breed stronger, more valuable planets, and auction their planets to the universe.

Takeaways

  • To quote the CoLab, "Prototype as if you are right, listen as if you are wrong." It is important to not be married to your ideas at any point in the process because openness enables quick iteration and improvement.
  • Build to learn. When things seem confusing, it is important to keep pushing. Embodying a question or unknown into a prototype helps you expand your understanding, even if it ends up not being the “right” prototype.
  • Narrow in on a small part of a large system.It is tempting to dive into a complex system to try and figure out how it works. It is more realistic and worthwhile to narrow in on a small section of the system and solve problems within that space despite the ambiguity that surrounds it.
