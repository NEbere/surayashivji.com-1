Events for iMessage

Role: UX Design, iOS Development

Tools: Sketch, Principle, Swift

Find mutually available times from iMessage.

Problem

We’ve all been part of this conversation.

How do friends communicate?

Group messaging. You have the freedom to communicate what you want, when you want, and to multiple people at once. Group messages, however, are an archive of thoughts and are not optimized for finding a mutual time to get together. Alternate scheduling services such as WhenIsGood or Doodle are used for meetings and interviews but feel out of place in a social context.

How might we help people schedule casual events with their friends in a frictionless way?

Research

I began by conducting secondary research on the usage of mobile group messaging, the ecosystem of messaging apps, and the demographic clusters within these apps. Pew Research Center reported that 42% of smartphone owners ages 18 to 29 uses messaging apps such as WhatsApp, iMessage, Kik or Messenger. As such, I chose to focus on people aged 15 to 30 as the demographic for this project. This chart displays users of any age who use a messaging app via mobile phone at least once per month.

I conducted 12 casual, in-person interviews in order to gain insight on the ways in which different friend groups use group messaging, whether or not people put in the effort to spend time with their friends, and the roadblocks people face in trying to do so.

The user interviews allowed me to identify patterns within my demographic, which I synthesized into three key insights that set up a clear journey of evidence for the remainder of my design process.

Design Process

iMessage Extensions

The user research insights showed that people love using group messages to chat, but find them tedious to plan events in due to their back and forth nature. At the same time, creating events should feel casual, simple, and easy. For these reasons, I decided that using an iMessage extension would be the best form as opposed to a stand-alone, external app. An iMessage app feels casual, convenient, and requires less work from the users, supporting the insights that were gleaned from my interviews.

Interaction Design

The user interviews validated my assumption that existing scheduling apps feel out of context in a casual setting; more importantly, they suggested that the process for a user to actually fill out his/her availability is what is currently most tedious. So, the first challenge was creating a simple, minimal input interaction for the user to enter his/her availability.

I explored input interactions such as automated suggestions, manual suggestions, as well as grid-based date and time selections. I ultimately decided to use a 'painting' touch gesture so that users can easily paint their availability on the calendar. Though it is sometimes necessary to create custom controls, using a pre-established interaction is favorable in this case as the unique value in this app does not lie in the calendar by itself.

The 'painting' gesture matches the fluid flow of a group chat.

Upon release, the circle cursor morphs into a square to solidify the user’s selection.

Constraints

Creating an app that lives within the context of a Messages conversation introduces unique design constraints. I had to work around the lack of screen real estate and use Apple’s HIG to think about the existing visual elements that users expect to see within iMessage. In order to balance visual appeal and usability, I decided to have the person initiating the event select a time frame from which the rest of the group can respond to as opposed to displaying all possible hours. This tradeoff sacrifices the flexibility that other scheduling services provide but makes sense for the light-weight, casual events that this app is designed to create.

Visual Design

I used a step-by-step flow for the availability and event creation in order to keep things simple. Some other questions that I tackled were:

  • Will the inviter’s view look different than the invitee’s view? (only where necessary).
  • Should people “paint” when they are available, or when they are busy? (available).
  • Will overlaying existing calendar events (iCal) feel out of place for a casual, friends-based event app? (No, it’s important to see all obligations to have an accurate schedule).
  • How should people’s responses be visually differentiated? (Stacked opacities of different colors).
  • Should we display the hours before or after the event is created? (Before, as the time of day is important for any get together).

Once the “inviter” sends his/her availability, the event is created. Now, all participants have the option of adding integrations to the event. Though I experimented with multiple 3rd party integrations that make sense for events, I narrowed it down to the following 4 for simplicity and clarity:

  1. Location: Suggest and vote for the eventʼs location with Maps.
  2. Music: Add a shared music playlist through either Apple Music or Spotify with your friendsʼ preferences in the mix.
  3. Photos: Create and be automatically subscribed to a shared album with iCloud Photo Sharing.
  4. Money: Send or receive money from individuals through Apple Pay in iOS 11.

Outcome

Events for iMessage allows friends to find mutually available times and organize integrated events all from iMessage.

Start the conversation.

Checking availability and planning events have always been a conversation. From the simple "I miss you, let's hang out" text to sharing availabilities and creating a Calendar linked event, Events for iMessage makes this conversation digital.

Make it an event.

Events for iMessage brings both casual and professional event planning directly to iMessage. Want to set up a mutually available interview time? Meet up with old friends for dinner? Suggest events as an organizer and send your own availability as an attendee. Share the event to Calendar with just a few swipes.

Takeaways

  • Respect the platform that you're designing for. It's important to understand the user's expectations and use established interaction patterns in order to design an experience that can live within the context of a Messages conversation.
  • Provide focused content. Messaging extensions should have a single focus. For example, there are countless potential integrations for an events app such as music playlists, payment services, social media, reminders, etc. However, for clarity and ease of use, such integrations should either be optional, limited, or eliminated all together. This is an area of the app I could definitely improve upon through additional user research.
